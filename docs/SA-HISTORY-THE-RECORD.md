## Editorial Note

South Africa's history spans at minimum 200,000 years of human habitation. This document covers three periods through which law, land, economy, and the lives of people were radically shaped:

- **Before colonisation and codified racial law** — the pre-colonial societies, the arrival of Europeans, and the colonial era up to Union (1910)
- **The apartheid era** — formal racial rule from the Natives Land Act (1913) through to the first democratic election (1994)
- **The post-apartheid era** — from 1994 to today, what changed and what has not

## PART ONE: BEFORE — The Land, People, and Laws Before Racial Rule

### 1.1 The First Inhabitants

The oldest evidence of human life on the southern tip of Africa dates back approximately 200,000 years. The **San people** (also called Bushmen) are among the oldest continuous human populations on Earth. They were hunter-gatherers who lived in small mobile groups, knew the land intimately, and left extraordinary rock art across the subcontinent — some of the oldest art in human history.

The **Khoikhoi** (also called Khoekhoe, meaning "people people") were pastoralists — they herded cattle and sheep. Unlike the San, they were not purely nomadic but moved seasonally with their herds. The Khoikhoi occupied much of the western Cape and coastal areas. Both the San and Khoikhoi are collectively called the **Khoisan** — the original inhabitants of what is now South Africa.

Neither the San nor the Khoikhoi had a concept of individual land ownership. Land was communally held, managed through kinship structures, and tied to seasonal movement. This distinction — between communal use rights and individual freehold ownership — would later become one of the central fault lines of colonisation.

### 1.2 The Bantu-Speaking Migrations and Kingdoms

From approximately the 3rd century CE onwards, Bantu-speaking agricultural communities migrated southward from central and west Africa. These were not a single people but many distinct nations, languages, and political structures.

They brought with them:
- Iron-working technology
- Agricultural practices (sorghum, millet)
- Cattle herding
- Settled village life
- Complex political and social organisation

By the time Europeans arrived in significant numbers in the 17th century, the interior of southern Africa was home to well-established kingdoms and confederacies:

**The Kingdom of Mapungubwe (c. 900–1300 CE)** — Located at the confluence of the Limpopo and Shashe rivers, Mapungubwe was the first known complex society in southern Africa. Archaeological evidence confirms it traded with Arabia, India, and China — gold, ivory, and glass beads have been recovered. It had a class-stratified society with a ruling elite buried on a hilltop separate from commoners. Climate change caused its abandonment around 1300 CE.

**The Kingdom of Zimbabwe** — Emerging from Mapungubwe's legacy, Great Zimbabwe was a major stone-walled city that controlled gold and ivory trade routes. Its descendants influenced political structures across the subcontinent.

**The Zulu Kingdom** — In the early 19th century, King Shaka reorganised the Zulu people into a centralised military state. Through a combination of military innovation (the short stabbing spear, the chest-and-horns battle formation) and political consolidation, the Zulu became the dominant power in what is now KwaZulu-Natal.

**The Sotho Kingdom** — King Moshoeshoe I consolidated Sotho-speaking people in the Drakensberg mountains (present-day Lesotho), creating a stable kingdom that successfully resisted both Zulu expansion and later Boer encroachment.

**The Xhosa-speaking chiefdoms** — Extended across the eastern Cape, organised around hereditary chiefs, cattle-based wealth, and sophisticated systems of law and governance transmitted orally.

**The Swazi Kingdom** — Founded in the early 19th century, occupying territory straddling present-day Swaziland (Eswatini) and Mpumalanga.

**Land in pre-colonial African society** was not owned individually in the European legal sense. Land was held by the community, allocated by chiefs or elders for use, and passed through lineage. The concept of selling land permanently to another person — particularly an outsider — was not part of indigenous legal systems. This fundamental difference in the concept of land would be exploited systematically during colonisation.

### 1.3 European Arrival and the Colonial Period (1488–1910)

**1488:** Portuguese explorer Bartolomeu Dias rounds the southern tip of Africa, naming it Cabo das Tormentas (Cape of Storms). It was later renamed the Cape of Good Hope.

**1652:** The Dutch East India Company (VOC — Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie) establishes a refreshment station at the Cape of Good Hope under Jan van Riebeeck. This was not intended as a colony but as a waystation for ships travelling between Europe and Asia.

The station became a colony as settlers (Boers — Dutch for "farmers") spread outward. The VOC imported slaves from Angola, Mozambique, Madagascar, and Southeast Asia to provide labour for the Cape economy. By 1700 there were more slaves in the Cape than free settlers.

**The destruction of Khoikhoi society:** VOC expansion required land and labour. Khoikhoi herding land was seized for settler farms. Khoikhoi who resisted were met with violence. VOC-introduced diseases — against which the Khoikhoi had no immunity — devastated their population. By the early 18th century, Khoikhoi society as an independent organised force was effectively destroyed. Survivors became farm labourers in conditions of near-serfdom.

**The San were hunted.** Colonial commandos (armed settler militias) systematically killed San people who resisted dispossession or raided settler livestock (often reclaiming what had been taken). The San were driven into the Kalahari and other marginal lands.

**1795 and 1806:** Britain seizes the Cape Colony from the Dutch (Batavian Republic), first temporarily in 1795, then permanently in 1806 following the Napoleonic Wars. British rule brought new settlers and a different administrative culture.

**1834:** Britain abolishes slavery throughout its empire, including the Cape Colony. This was a turning point: the approximately 36,000 enslaved people in the Cape were freed. Slave owners received compensation — the enslaved people did not.

The abolition of slavery directly contributed to the **Great Trek (1835–1846)**: Boer settlers (Voortrekkers) moved northward and eastward in large numbers to escape British law and governance, which they believed undermined their social order and threatened their access to cheap labour. They entered territories occupied by Zulu, Ndebele, Sotho, and other peoples, leading to a series of wars.

**1838: The Battle of Blood River** — Voortrekkers defeat a Zulu force, a pivotal moment in Boer mythology and later in apartheid ideology.

**1867 and 1886: Diamonds and gold.** Diamond deposits are discovered near Kimberley (1867) and the world's largest gold reef is found on the Witwatersrand (1886). These discoveries transformed South Africa from a marginal agricultural colony into one of the most economically significant territories on earth. They triggered:
- A rush of immigrants from Britain and across the world
- Industrial-scale mining requiring massive cheap labour
- The Anglo-Boer Wars (1880–1881 and 1899–1902) as Britain and Boer republics fought for control of this extraordinary wealth

**1902: Peace of Vereeniging** ends the Second Anglo-Boer War. Britain wins. The Boer republics (Transvaal and Orange Free State) become British territories.

**1910: Union of South Africa** — The four colonies (Cape, Natal, Transvaal, Orange Free State) unite into the Union of South Africa under British dominion. The political settlement deliberately excludes the vast majority of the population: only white men can vote in most provinces. The Cape had a limited property-based franchise that included some black and coloured men — this would be removed later.

### 1.4 The Laws That Built Racial Exclusion (Pre-Apartheid: 1910–1948)

Before formal apartheid began in 1948, the legal architecture of racial exclusion was already being constructed:

| Year | Law | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| 1910 | South Africa Act (Union) | Excludes black people from Parliament. Reserves Cape Qualified Franchise — later removed. |
| 1911 | Mines and Works Act | Reserves skilled mining jobs ("blasting certificates") for white workers. Colour bar in mining industry. |
| **1913** | **Natives Land Act 27 of 1913** | **Restricts black land ownership to 7.3% of South Africa's total land area. Prohibits black people from buying or leasing land outside designated "reserves." Immediately displaces hundreds of thousands.** |
| 1923 | Natives (Urban Areas) Act | Controls movement of black people into cities. Creates the legal basis for townships — black people required to live separately from white areas. |
| 1927 | Immorality Act | Prohibits sexual relations between white and black people. |
| 1927 | Native Administration Act | Gives executive government sweeping power to govern "native reserves" by proclamation (bypassing Parliament). |
| 1936 | Representation of Natives Act | Removes black voters from the common voters' roll in the Cape. They can now only elect three white MPs to "represent" them. |
| 1936 | Native Trust and Land Act | Expands reserve areas to approximately 13% — still a fraction of the country's land for the majority of its people. |
| 1946 | Asiatic Land Tenure Act | Restricts land ownership and residence rights of Indian South Africans. |

**The Natives Land Act of 1913 is the single most consequential law in South African history.** Its effects are still visible today. It:
- Immediately displaced sharecroppers and tenant farmers who had been living on white-owned land
- Prevented black families from accumulating land-based wealth — the primary form of intergenerational wealth in all agrarian societies
- Forced black men into the labour market — particularly the mines — because subsistence farming on reserves was impossible
- Created the geographic separation of races that apartheid would later formalise and intensify

Sol Plaatje, a founder of the African National Congress and a journalist, documented the immediate human cost in his 1916 book *Native Life in South Africa*: "Awakening on Friday morning, June 20, 1913, the South African native found himself, not actually a slave, but a pariah in the land of his birth."

## PART TWO: DURING — The Apartheid State (1948–1994)

### 2.1 How Apartheid Came to Power

In May 1948, the National Party under D.F. Malan won the general election — an election in which only white people could vote. The National Party had campaigned explicitly on *apartheid* (Afrikaans for "separateness"). They won with 70 seats against the United Party's 65 — a narrow majority that would shape the next 46 years.

The National Party represented primarily Afrikaner interests. Many of its leaders had been influenced by European fascism in the 1930s. Their ideology combined Calvinist theology (which some used to justify racial hierarchy), Afrikaner nationalism, and the economic interests of white farmers and workers.

They inherited the racial laws already on the books and proceeded to systematise, deepen, and extend them into a total system of control.

### 2.2 The Laws of Apartheid — A Complete Record

**Classification and Identity**

| Year | Law | What It Did |
|---|---|---|
| 1950 | Population Registration Act 30 of 1950 | Every South African classified by race: Native (Black), Coloured, or White. Asian (Indian) category added later. Identity documents carried racial classification. Families were sometimes split — classified differently. |
| 1949 | Prohibition of Mixed Marriages Act 55 of 1949 | Marriage between white and non-white people made illegal. |
| 1950 | Immorality Amendment Act 21 of 1950 | Extended the 1927 Immorality Act — prohibited sexual relations between white and any non-white person. Police enforced this by raiding homes and beds. |

**Land and Housing**

| Year | Law | What It Did |
|---|---|---|
| 1950 | Group Areas Act 41 of 1950 | Divided every urban area into racial zones. Non-white people in "wrong" zones were forcibly removed. Led to the destruction of communities including Sophiatown (Johannesburg), District Six (Cape Town), and Cato Manor (Durban). |
| 1951 | Prevention of Illegal Squatting Act 52 of 1951 | Gave the Minister of Native Affairs power to remove black people from public or private land and establish resettlement camps. |
| 1954 | Natives Resettlement Act | Specifically authorised the removal of black people from the Western Areas of Johannesburg — targeting Sophiatown. |
| 1970 | Bantu Homelands Citizenship Act | Stripped every black South African of their South African citizenship. All black people were declared citizens of one of 10 "homelands" (Bantustans), regardless of where they actually lived. Many had never set foot in their designated "homeland." |

**Movement and Labour**

| Year | Law | What It Did |
|---|---|---|
| 1952 | Natives (Abolition of Passes) Act 67 of 1952 | Despite its name, this Act intensified rather than abolished the pass system. Required all black men (later extended to women) over 16 to carry a "reference book" (passbook) at all times. Could not move, work, or be in an urban area without a valid pass. Failure to produce a pass was a criminal offence. |
| 1953 | Native Labour (Settlement of Disputes) Act | Prohibited black workers from striking. |
| 1956 | Industrial Conciliation Act | Defined "employee" in a way that excluded black workers from many labour protections. Reserved skilled jobs for white workers in many sectors. |

**Education**

| Year | Law | What It Did |
|---|---|---|
| 1953 | Bantu Education Act 47 of 1953 | Transferred control of black education from missions and provinces to the central government. Deliberately provided inferior education. Hendrik Verwoerd, then Minister of Native Affairs, stated the purpose explicitly: "There is no place for the Bantu in the European community above the level of certain forms of labour." Per pupil spending ratio: white children received approximately 10 times more than black children at the height of apartheid. |
| 1959 | Extension of University Education Act | Established separate universities for each race group. Prohibited black students from attending "white" universities without special ministerial permission. Created "bush colleges" for black students with inferior resources. |

**Political Suppression**

| Year | Law | What It Did |
|---|---|---|
| 1950 | Suppression of Communism Act 44 of 1950 | Defined "communism" so broadly that virtually any opposition to apartheid could be classified as communist. Gave government power to "ban" individuals — preventing them from attending meetings, being quoted in print, or leaving their homes. |
| 1959 | Promotion of Bantu Self-Government Act | Abolished what remained of black parliamentary representation. Created the fiction of "self-government" in Bantustans. |
| 1960 | Unlawful Organisations Act | Banned the African National Congress and the Pan Africanist Congress following the Sharpeville Massacre. |
| 1967 | Terrorism Act 83 of 1967 | Allowed detention without trial for 14 days (later extended indefinitely). "Terrorism" defined so broadly it covered most forms of resistance. |
| 1968 | Prohibition of Political Interference Act | Banned multiracial political parties. |

**Amenities and Daily Life**

The Reservation of Separate Amenities Act 49 of 1953 mandated separate and invariably unequal public facilities: beaches, parks, libraries, hospitals, buses, trains, toilets, restaurants, cinemas, and more. The facilities designated for non-white people were consistently inferior.

This was the daily, grinding reality of apartheid — not just the grand laws of land and citizenship, but the humiliation built into every public space.

### 2.3 The Bantustans — The Fiction of Separate Nations

One of the most cynical features of apartheid was the creation of 10 "Bantustans" or "homelands" — nominally self-governing territories allocated to different black ethnic groups. Four were declared "independent": Transkei (1976), Bophuthatswana (1977), Venda (1979), and Ciskei (1981).

These declarations of independence were recognised by no country in the world other than South Africa.

**What the Bantustans actually were:**
- Overcrowded, resource-poor territories comprising approximately 13% of South Africa's land for over 70% of its population
- Economically unviable by design — dependent on migrant labour flowing to white South Africa
- Tools for stripping black South Africans of citizenship and the political rights that came with it
- Administered by compliant chiefs appointed or supported by Pretoria
- Sites of severe poverty, malnutrition, and underdevelopment

The Bantustans were not separate countries — they were labour reserves dressed in the language of self-determination.

### 2.4 The Economy Under Apartheid

Apartheid was not only a system of racial oppression — it was an economic system that transferred enormous wealth from black people to white people, and from black labour to white capital.

**The mining economy:** The gold and diamond mines that drove South Africa's economy were built on black migrant labour. Black miners were paid a fraction of white miners' wages — legally mandated by the Mines and Works Act's colour bar. Black miners lived in all-male hostels, separated from their families in the Bantustans. They could not hold skilled or supervisory positions. Between 1911 and 1970, the real wages of black miners barely changed while the profitability of the mines multiplied.

**Manufacturing and industry:** As South Africa industrialised through the mid-20th century, Job Reservation laws directed skilled positions to white workers. Black workers could be employed as semi-skilled or unskilled labour — but were legally prevented from advancing. The wage gap between white and black workers was among the largest in the world.

**Agriculture:** Most South African farmland was white-owned. Black farm workers were tied to farms through a system resembling feudalism — they received housing and some food in exchange for labour, with limited ability to leave or negotiate. The 1913 Land Act had destroyed black smallholder farming by removing the land base on which it depended.

**Apartheid GDP statistics (verified):**
- In 1970, white South Africans (approximately 17% of the population at the time) earned 74% of all income generated in South Africa
- Black South Africans (approximately 70% of the population) earned approximately 19% of all income
- The white-to-black per capita income ratio stood at approximately 15:1 in the early 1970s

**The international economy and sanctions:**
- By the 1980s, international sanctions and divestment campaigns began to affect South Africa's economy
- The rand weakened significantly — from approximately R1.20 to the US dollar in 1980 to R2.80 by 1985
- International banks refused to roll over South African debt in 1985
- Economic pressure, combined with internal resistance, contributed to the system's eventual collapse

### 2.5 The People — Resistance, Suffering, and Survival

**The African National Congress (ANC)** was founded in 1912 — one year before the Natives Land Act, in direct response to the direction the new Union was taking. For its first 48 years it pursued peaceful protest, petitions, and delegations to Britain. It was banned in 1960.

**The Sharpeville Massacre (21 March 1960):** Police opened fire on a crowd of approximately 5,000 people who had gathered at Sharpeville police station without their passbooks as a peaceful protest. 69 people were killed — most shot in the back as they fled. 186 were wounded. The massacre triggered international condemnation and hardened positions on both sides.

**Robben Island:** After the Rivonia Trial (1963–64), Nelson Mandela and seven others were sentenced to life imprisonment. Mandela served 27 years — 18 of them on Robben Island, a prison on an island in Table Bay where prisoners quarried lime in conditions designed to break them.

**The Soweto Uprising (16 June 1976):** Students in Soweto rose up against the government's requirement that Afrikaans be used as the medium of instruction in black schools. Police fired on students. The death toll is officially recorded at 176 — other estimates are higher. Hector Pieterson, a 13-year-old boy, was among the first killed. The image of his body being carried became one of the defining photographs of the apartheid era. June 16 is now Youth Day in South Africa.

**Steve Biko (1946–1977):** Founder of the Black Consciousness Movement, Biko was arrested, tortured in detention, and died of brain injuries inflicted by security police. He was 30 years old. His death brought global attention to South Africa's treatment of political prisoners. The officer responsible was never convicted.

**The women who carried passes:** When the government extended the pass laws to women in 1956, 20,000 women marched to the Union Buildings in Pretoria in protest. They left bundles of passes on the steps. They sang: *"Wathint' abafazi, wathint' imbokodo"* — "You strike a woman, you strike a rock."

**The State of Emergency (1985–1990):** As internal resistance intensified in the 1980s, the government declared a State of Emergency giving security forces sweeping powers of detention without trial. Thousands were detained. Torture was systematic. The security apparatus carried out assassinations of activists inside and outside South Africa.

### 2.6 The End of Apartheid (1990–1994)

By the late 1980s, apartheid's position was untenable: economic sanctions were biting, internal resistance was intensifying, and the Cold War context that had made South Africa a Western ally was ending with the fall of the Soviet Union.

**February 2, 1990:** President F.W. de Klerk announces the unbanning of the ANC, PAC, Communist Party, and other organisations, and the release of political prisoners.

**February 11, 1990:** Nelson Mandela walks free after 27 years in prison.

**1990–1993:** Negotiations at Codesa (Convention for a Democratic South Africa) between the ANC, National Party, and other parties. The process was violent — the Inkatha Freedom Party and elements of the security forces killed thousands during the negotiation period — but ultimately produced agreement.

**1993:** The interim Constitution is adopted, establishing universal adult suffrage.

**April 27, 1994:** The first democratic election. South Africans of all races vote for the first time. Nelson Mandela is elected President. The ANC wins 62.65% of the vote.

## PART THREE: AFTER — Post-Apartheid South Africa (1994–2026)

### 3.1 The Laws of Post-Apartheid South Africa

**The Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996** is the supreme law. It is widely regarded as one of the most progressive constitutions in the world. It:
- Establishes universal adult suffrage
- Contains a comprehensive Bill of Rights (Chapter 2)
- Explicitly recognises the need to remedy past injustice through Section 9(2) — the equality clause
- Protects rights to housing (§26), healthcare, food and water (§27), education (§29), and land (§25)
- Protects freedom of expression, association, religion, movement, and privacy
- Establishes independent institutions including the Public Protector, Human Rights Commission, and Auditor-General

**Key post-apartheid legislation:**

| Year | Law | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1994 | Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994 | Enables people dispossessed of land since 1913 to claim restitution or compensation |
| 1994 | Electoral Act | Establishes the framework for free and fair elections |
| 1995 | Promotion of National Unity and Reconciliation Act 34 of 1995 | Creates the Truth and Reconciliation Commission |
| 1996 | Constitution of the Republic of South Africa | Supreme law — replaces all apartheid-era legislation |
| 1996 | Extension of Security of Tenure Act | Protects farm workers and labour tenants from eviction |
| 1996 | Land Reform (Labour Tenants) Act | Protects people who work for farmers in exchange for right to occupy land |
| 1997 | Basic Conditions of Employment Act | Minimum standards for all workers — minimum wage, leave, etc. |
| 1998 | Employment Equity Act 55 of 1998 | Prohibits unfair discrimination in employment; requires equity plans from large employers |
| 1998 | Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 | Combats organised crime and money laundering |
| 1998 | National Prosecuting Authority Act 32 of 1998 | Creates the independent NPA |
| 1999 | Public Finance Management Act 1 of 1999 | Governs how public money is spent — the law whose violation underlies most corruption stories on this platform |
| 2000 | Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act | Extends equality protection beyond the workplace |
| 2000 | Promotion of Access to Information Act | Right to access information held by state or private bodies |
| 2003 | B-BBEE Act 53 of 2003 | Framework for economic transformation |
| 2004 | Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act 12 of 2004 (PRECCA) | Makes corruption a criminal offence; duty to report |
| 2013 | Protection of Personal Information Act 4 of 2013 (POPIA) | Data protection rights for all South Africans |

**Laws repealed after apartheid:**
- Natives Land Act (repealed 1991 by Abolition of Racially Based Land Measures Act)
- Population Registration Act (repealed 1991)
- Group Areas Act (repealed 1991)
- Pass Laws (repealed 1986)
- Prohibition of Mixed Marriages Act (repealed 1985)
- Immorality Act (repealed 1985)
- Bantu Education Act (repealed 1979, replaced with inferior legislation, eventually replaced by post-1994 framework)
- Suppression of Communism Act (repealed 1990)
- All homeland citizenship legislation

### 3.2 Land After Apartheid — The Promise and the Reality

**What was promised:** The Constitution (Section 25) guarantees the right to property and commits the state to land reform through redistribution, restitution, and tenure reform. The ANC government committed to redistributing 30% of white-owned agricultural land by 2014.

**What happened:**
- The "willing seller, willing buyer" model was adopted — the state would buy land from white owners at market value and redistribute it. Critics argued market prices made this prohibitively slow.
- By 2018, approximately 10% of agricultural land had been redistributed — not 30%
- The land restitution process received over 80,000 claims. Many were settled with financial compensation rather than land
- Title deed backlogs: over 1 million RDP houses were built without the occupants receiving legal title deeds — leaving people unable to sell, borrow against, or legally inherit their homes
- White South Africans still own the majority of South Africa's agricultural land

**The Section 25 debate:** In 2017–2019, Parliament debated amending Section 25 of the Constitution to explicitly allow expropriation of land without compensation. The Ad Hoc Committee on Section 25 (seeded in The Record) failed to pass the amendment with the required two-thirds majority before Parliament was dissolved in 2024.

**The current position (2026):** The land question remains unresolved. Land reform has been slow by any measure. The debate about expropriation without compensation continues. No constitutional amendment has been passed.

### 3.3 The Economy After Apartheid — What Changed and What Did Not

**What improved:**
- Access to electricity: the electrification programme connected millions of households previously without power — from approximately 36% of households in 1994 to over 85% by the early 2010s
- Access to water: millions of households gained access to piped water for the first time
- Social grants: 18 million South Africans now receive government grants — the largest social protection programme in sub-Saharan Africa
- The black middle class grew significantly — from negligible in 1994 to approximately 4–5 million people by the 2010s
- The white-to-black income ratio fell from approximately 15:1 in the early 1990s to approximately 9.3:1 by 2019

**What did not change or worsened:**

The World Inequality Database's research covering 1993 to 2019 found that the decline in racial income inequality was driven almost entirely by a surge in top black incomes — the growth of a black elite — rather than increased wealth for the bottom 50% of the population.

The Southern Centre for Inequality Studies found no evidence that wealth inequality (as opposed to income) decreased since the end of apartheid. Asset allocations established before 1993 — farms, businesses, shares, property — continued to shape wealth inequality.

**Verified statistics (2024/2025):**

| Measure | Figure | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Gini coefficient | 0.63 — highest in the world | World Bank |
| Official unemployment rate | 31.9% (Q3 2025) | Stats SA |
| Expanded unemployment (including discouraged workers) | 42.4% | Stats SA Q3 2025 |
| Youth unemployment (15–24) | Over 60% | Stats SA |
| Population below lower-bound poverty line (R1,300/month per person) | 23.2 million people (37.9%) | Stats SA |
| Population in extreme poverty (below R777/month) | Approximately 11 million | Stats SA |
| Median black household wealth | R70,000 | Southern Centre for Inequality Studies / Wits |
| Median white household wealth | R1.36 million | Same source |
| White-to-black median wealth ratio | ~19:1 | Same source |
| White people's share of top 10% income | Still disproportionate | World Inequality Database |
| Top 10% share of all wealth | 86% | World Bank 2022 |
| Income: white average vs black average | White workers earn approximately 3x black workers | CNN / Stats SA |
| Land: agricultural land white-owned | Majority — exact current figure disputed | PLAAS research |
| Real per capita GDP growth | Negative since approximately 2014 | World Bank |

**The racial wealth gap in perspective:** The white-to-black income ratio in the United States — itself a country with a deep history of racial economic exclusion — stands at approximately 1.3. South Africa's ratio of 9.3 (income) and approximately 19 (wealth) indicates that apartheid's economic legacy is of a different order of magnitude from other systems of racial inequality.

### 3.4 The People After Apartheid — Progress and Persistent Gaps

**What changed:**
- Black South Africans now vote, hold public office, lead government, run major corporations, sit on the Constitutional Court, and exercise civil and political rights denied to their parents and grandparents
- The TRC (1996–1998) provided a formal process for naming apartheid-era violations and, in limited cases, granting amnesty to perpetrators who made full disclosure — a globally novel approach to transitional justice
- The human rights architecture — the Public Protector, Human Rights Commission, Constitutional Court — provided genuine accountability mechanisms unknown under apartheid

**What persisted:**
- The racial wealth gap is structural and intergenerational — it cannot be closed in one or two generations without active redistribution
- Townships built under apartheid to house black workers on the periphery of cities remain. The geography of apartheid persists in city layouts. The commute from township to work is still the daily reality for millions
- The education gap created by Bantu Education has not been repaired. Schools in former "black areas" remain under-resourced relative to former "white schools," now often called Model C schools. The results persist in the racial profile of university graduates and skilled workers
- Informal networks of professional advantage — who you know, which schools you went to, who can vouch for you — remain racially skewed. This is the lived reality behind the phrase: "If they cannot pronounce your name, they will not make you a manager"
- The Gini coefficient has not improved since 1994 — inequality within racial groups has increased as a small black elite has grown wealthy while the majority remain poor

**The TRC's unfinished business:** The TRC recommended that perpetrators denied amnesty face prosecution. Approximately 30 years later, almost none have been prosecuted. This is the reason President Ramaphosa established the TRC Prosecutions Inquiry in 2025 — which is an active investigation tracked on this platform.

## PART FIVE: THE CONNECTING THREAD

The history of South Africa is a single thread with three phases:

**1. Dispossession** — over 250 years, land, labour, education, movement, and political voice were taken from the majority of the population through law and force.

**2. Resistance and Liberation** — the majority fought back, at enormous cost, and ultimately won political freedom in 1994.

**3. The unfinished question** — political freedom arrived in 1994. Economic freedom has not. The laws of apartheid were repealed. The wealth they transferred was not.

The Gini coefficient — the measure of inequality — has not improved since 1994. The racial wealth gap at the median level has not closed. The farmland is still predominantly in the hands of the descendants of those who received it through the Natives Land Act and its successors. The township geography of apartheid shapes where people live and how far they commute.

This is not a counsel of despair. Three and a half million households have been electrified. Eighteen million people receive social grants. The Constitutional Court has protected rights that the apartheid state extinguished. The press can report. The Public Protector can investigate. Courts can hold presidents accountable.

But the gap between political equality — now 30 years old — and economic equality remains South Africa's defining challenge.

Every story on The Record — the corruption in housing tenders, the money stolen from hospitals, the police commissioner accused of protecting criminal networks, the SIU recovering billions from state capture, the commissions investigating what went wrong — connects to this history.

When money is stolen from a housing project, it is stolen from people who have already waited 30 years for a house that was promised when apartheid ended. When money is stolen from school maintenance, it is stolen from children who already attend under-resourced schools — the inheritance of Bantu Education. When water infrastructure is allowed to collapse, the people without water are disproportionately the same people who were told, under apartheid, that clean water was not for them.

The history is not the past. It is the context in which every present event takes place.
